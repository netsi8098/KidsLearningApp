# Kids Learning Fun -- Copy Bible

The definitive writer's guide to every word in Kids Learning Fun.
This document defines who we are, how we speak, and the standards every
piece of copy must meet.

---

## 1. Brand Voice Overview

### Who We Are

Kids Learning Fun is a premium, parent-trusted, child-loved learning app for
ages 2--8. We are the warm, encouraging friend who makes learning feel like play.
We are never patronizing, never rushed, and never robotic.

Every word in this app is a tiny act of care.

### Who We Are Talking To

We serve two audiences simultaneously:

- **Children (ages 2--8)**: They experience the app directly. They hear our words
  read aloud, see them on screen, and feel them emotionally. Our words shape how
  they feel about learning itself.

- **Parents/Caregivers**: They see our words on the settings screen, the parent
  dashboard, and occasionally over their child's shoulder. Our words build trust
  and demonstrate that we understand child development.

### Our Promise

- We never make a child feel bad for trying.
- We never use the word "wrong."
- We celebrate effort as much as results.
- We speak clearly, warmly, and with purpose.
- We sound like a favorite teacher, not a software product.

## 2. Child-Facing Tone Guide

### Core Principles

1. **Clarity before cleverness.** If a child cannot understand the sentence
   on first hearing, rewrite it.
2. **Warmth is not weakness.** Warm language builds confidence. Confidence
   builds learners.
3. **One idea per sentence.** Do not stack instructions.
4. **Active voice always.** "Tap the star" not "The star should be tapped."
5. **Present tense.** "You found it" not "You have successfully located the item."

### By Age Group

| Rule | Ages 2--3 | Ages 4--5 | Ages 6--8 |
|------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Max words per sentence | 8 | 12 | 16 |
| Max syllables per word | 2 | 3 | 4 |
| Questions per screen | 1 | 1 | 1--2 |
| Exclamation points per line | 0--1 | 0--1 | 0--1 |
| Can use "because" | No | Yes (simple) | Yes |
| Abstract concepts | No | Emerging | Yes |

### Tone by Context

**Learning mode** -- Encouraging, clear, steady.

> "This is the letter A. Can you say A?"

**Play mode** -- Playful, energetic, celebratory.

> "Whoosh! You matched them all!"

**Bedtime mode** -- Gentle, calm, no exclamation points.

> "The stars are coming out, one by one."

**Reward mode** -- Proud, specific, warm.

> "You found all the circles. Sharp eyes!"

### Words We Never Use

| Never | Instead use |
|-------|------------|
| Wrong | "Not quite" / "Let's try again" |
| Incorrect | "Almost" / "Not that one" |
| Fail / failed | "Nice try" / "So close" |
| Bad / terrible | (remove entirely) |
| Stupid / dumb | (remove entirely) |
| Hurry / hurry up | "Take your time" |
| Easy / simple | (remove -- do not label difficulty) |
| Hard / difficult | "Tricky" / "A fun challenge" |
| Wrong answer | "Not quite -- let's look again" |

## 3. Parent-Facing Tone Guide

### Core Principles

1. **Data with warmth.** Lead with the number, follow with the context.
2. **No jargon.** Say "counting" not "numeracy skills." Say "reading" not
   "literacy competency."
3. **Never alarm.** Every insight is an opportunity, not a warning.
4. **Respect their time.** Keep it under 20 words per sentence.
5. **Trust-building.** Explain why, briefly.

### Dashboard Copy

Good:
> "12 activities completed this week -- up from 8 last week."

Bad:
> "Your child's engagement metrics indicate a 50% increase in session frequency."

### Settings Copy

Good:
> "When enabled, the app reads instructions aloud. Great for early learners
> still building reading skills."

Bad:
> "Toggles the TTS engine on/off."

### Insight Copy Pattern

```
[Number or fact]. [Brief context or next step].
```

Examples:
- "3 lessons this week. Counting is the current favorite."
- "Most active time: mornings between 8--9 AM."
- "Strong pattern in letter recognition. Next step: letter sounds."

## 4. Mascot Voice Personalities

Five mascots, five distinct voices. When a mascot speaks, every word must
sound like that specific character.

### Leo Lion -- The Warm Leader

- **Personality**: Brave, supportive, grounding.
- **Speech pattern**: Uses "we" and "together" often. Coaches gently.
- **Signature**: "ROAR!" (used sparingly, for big moments only).
- **Energy**: Steady warmth. The friend who holds your hand.
- **Catchphrases**:
  - "We can do this together."
  - "I believe in you."
  - "Let's take it one step at a time."

**Example dialogue:**

> "Hey there, friend! We are going to learn about colors today. I know you will
> do great -- you always do. Ready? Let's go together."

### Daisy Duck -- The Musical Creator

- **Personality**: Bubbly, artistic, sing-song.
- **Speech pattern**: Rhymes naturally. Notices beauty. Uses alliteration.
- **Signature**: "Quack!" (playful, not every line).
- **Energy**: Sparkle, not shouting. Gentle excitement.
- **Catchphrases**:
  - "La la la, what shall we make today?"
  - "Beautiful bright blue!"
  - "Oh my, look at what you created!"

**Example dialogue:**

> "Quack! Hello, little artist. See all these pretty colors? Pick your favorite
> and let's paint something beautiful. A little dab here, a little dab there...
> la la la!"

### Ollie Owl -- The Wise Thinker

- **Personality**: Thoughtful, calm, questioning.
- **Speech pattern**: Asks gentle questions. Shares facts with delight. Pauses to think.
- **Signature**: "Hoot hoot!" (warm, never loud).
- **Energy**: Measured and patient. The bedtime companion.
- **Catchphrases**:
  - "Hmm, I wonder..."
  - "Did you know...?"
  - "Take your time. Good things come to those who think."

**Example dialogue:**

> "Hoot hoot! Did you know that owls can turn their heads almost all the way
> around? Hmm... I wonder if you can turn YOUR head. Try it! Not quite as
> far, but that was wonderful."

### Ruby Rabbit -- The Energetic Adventurer

- **Personality**: Bouncy, action-oriented, unstoppable.
- **Speech pattern**: Action verbs. Short punchy sentences. Builds anticipation.
- **Signature**: "Hop hop!" (bursting with energy).
- **Energy**: Running alongside you, never ahead. Cheerleader energy.
- **Catchphrases**:
  - "Ready, set... GO!"
  - "Let's go!"
  - "Shake it off and try again!"

**Example dialogue:**

> "Hop hop! Ready to play a matching game? I bet you can find all the pairs.
> Ready... set... GO! Whoosh, you found one already! Keep going, keep going!"

### Finn Fox -- The Clever Explorer

- **Personality**: Creative, puzzle-loving, conspiratorial.
- **Speech pattern**: Riddle-like. Hints instead of answers. Frames learning as detective work.
- **Signature**: "Pssst..." (conspiratorial whisper).
- **Energy**: The clever friend who respects your intelligence.
- **Catchphrases**:
  - "Pssst... I found something cool."
  - "Here is a puzzle for you..."
  - "Follow the clues!"

**Example dialogue:**

> "Pssst... come over here. I found something hidden in the forest. See those
> footprints? I think they belong to a... hmm, what do you think? Follow the
> clues and let's find out together!"

## 5. Copy Patterns for Every Screen Type

### Lesson Intro

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Header | "Let's learn about {topic}!" | Max 10 words. One punctuation mark. |
| Mascot bubble | 1--2 sentences in character voice | End with invitation. |
| Body | "In this lesson, you will learn {goal}." | One concrete outcome. |
| CTA | "Let's go!" | Max 3 words. |

### Activity / Instruction

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Instruction | "Tap the {target}." | Lead with action verb. One sentence. |
| Hint | "Hint: Look for something {adj}." | Guides, never gives answer. |
| Progress | "3 of 10" | Pure information. |

### Quiz Question

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Question | "{questionText}" | Clear, direct, no double negatives. |
| Correct | "Yes! That is right." | Immediate, warm, varied. |
| Incorrect | "Not quite. The answer is {answer}." | Never "wrong." Always reveal answer. |
| Completion | "Quiz done! {n} out of {total}." | Always positive framing. |

### Reward / Celebration

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Header | "You did it!" | Max 4 words. Bold. |
| Body | "You earned a {reward} for {achievement}." | Name both reward and reason. |
| Mascot | In-character celebration | Include signature sound for big wins. |
| CTA | "Keep going!" | Forward-looking. |

### Empty State

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Header | "Nothing here yet!" | Opportunity, not apology. |
| Body | "Start exploring to fill this up!" | One concrete action. |
| CTA | "Let's explore!" | Max 3 words. |

### Error State

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Header | "Oops!" | Friendly, never technical. |
| Body | "Something went sideways. Let us try again." | "We" language. No jargon. |
| CTA | "Try again" | Clear escape route. |

### Search (No Results)

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Header | "No results found" | Non-blaming. |
| Body | "Try different words." | Concrete next step. |
| Suggestion | "How about exploring {topic}?" | Keep them engaged. |

### Bedtime Transition

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Header | "Time to wind down..." | Ellipses. No exclamation. |
| Body | Nature imagery, lyrical. | Max 2 sentences. |
| Mascot | Ollie by default. Whisper energy. | Ends with reassurance. |
| CTA | "Start bedtime story" | Soft. No exclamation. |

### Pause / Break

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Header | "Brain Break!" | Celebrate the break. |
| Body | "You earned a rest." | Normalize breaks. |
| Suggestion | "Stand up and stretch!" | Safe physical action. |
| CTA | "I'm ready to come back!" | Child controls resume. |

### Story Narration

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Intro | "Once upon a time..." | Classic storybook opening. |
| Page turn | "Tap to turn the page." | Minimal -- stay in story. |
| Ending | "The End." | Satisfying closure. |
| Read-aloud | "Read to me" | Max 3 words. |

### Game Results

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Win header | "You won!" | Celebratory. Max 3 words. |
| Win body | "You scored {n} points!" | Compare to personal best only. |
| Incomplete header | "Nice try!" | NEVER "you lost." |
| Incomplete body | "You scored {n}. Try to beat it?" | Frame replay as opportunity. |

### Collection Overview

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Header | "{collectionName}" | Title case, no punctuation. |
| Description | Active language, max 15 words. | "Explore..." not "This contains..." |
| Progress | "{n} of {total} complete" | Show fraction. |
| 100% message | "You finished the whole collection!" | Name the effort. |

### Weekly Recap (Parent)

| Area | Pattern | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Header | "This Week's Learning" | Professional. |
| Summary | "{n} activities across {n} topics." | Lead with numbers. |
| Highlight | "Standout: {detail}." | Most notable win. |
| Suggestion | "Next week, try {topic}." | Gentle, never critical. |

## 6. Repetition Control Strategy

Children benefit from repetition, but stale copy feels robotic. We manage
freshness at three levels:

### Level 1: Variation Groups

Every phrase in the phrase bank belongs to a `variationGroup`. Phrases in the
same group are interchangeable. The system cycles through variants to avoid
repeating the same text on consecutive encounters.

Example group `effort-persistence`:
- "You didn't give up, and that's amazing."
- "You kept going even when it was tricky."
- "You stuck with it and I am so proud of you."

### Level 2: Recent-ID Tracking

The `pickFreshPhrase()` helper accepts an array of recently used phrase IDs.
It excludes those from the selection pool, guaranteeing no immediate repeats.
When all phrases in a pool have been used, the tracker resets.

### Level 3: Mascot Rotation

Different mascots deliver the same encouragement in different voices. Switching
mascots refreshes the copy even when the underlying message is similar.

- Leo: "We can do this together."
- Ruby: "Hop back up! You have got this."
- Ollie: "Take your time. There is no rush."

### Implementation Rule

No phrase should repeat within 5 consecutive encounters of the same context.
If a category has fewer than 5 phrases for an age group, add more.

## 7. Vocabulary Levels Reference

### Ages 2--3 (Toddlers)

- **Max word length**: 6 characters
- **Max syllables**: 2
- **Sentence length**: up to 8 words
- **Allowed concepts**: Concrete objects, basic actions, primary colors,
  simple shapes, family members, common animals
- **Forbidden**: Abstract concepts, conditional logic, multi-step instructions

### Ages 4--5 (Preschool)

- **Max word length**: 8 characters
- **Max syllables**: 3
- **Sentence length**: up to 12 words
- **Allowed concepts**: All of 2--3 plus: comparison, sequencing, simple
  cause/effect, feelings vocabulary, nature concepts
- **Forbidden**: Edu-jargon, complex conditionals, passive voice

### Ages 6--8 (Early Readers)

- **Max word length**: 10 characters
- **Max syllables**: 4
- **Sentence length**: up to 16 words
- **Allowed concepts**: All of 4--5 plus: predictions, strategies, abstract
  reasoning, world knowledge, self-reflection
- **Forbidden**: Technical jargon, SAT-level vocabulary, complex syntax

### Quick Substitution Reference

| Complex Word | Ages 2--3 | Ages 4--5 |
|---|---|---|
| discover | find | discover |
| explore | look at | explore |
| incredible | so good | amazing |
| continue | keep going | keep going |
| challenge | try | tricky one |
| collection | group | set |
| practice | do again | practice |
| remember | think of | remember |
| instructions | what to do | steps |

## 8. Conversation Rules

### Mascot vs. Narrator vs. Parent Text

The app uses three distinct "speakers." They should never be confused.

**Mascot speech** (MascotBubble component):
- First person: "I believe in you."
- Has personality, character voice, signature sounds.
- Appears in speech bubbles with the mascot's avatar.
- Written to the mascot's tone profile.

**Narrator text** (Headers, instructions, labels):
- Second person: "Tap the red circle."
- Neutral, clear, personality-free.
- Appears as screen headings, instructions, and labels.
- Written to the child-learning or child-play tone profile.

**Parent text** (Dashboard, Settings):
- Third person for child: "Your child completed 3 lessons."
- Professional, warm, data-driven.
- Appears only in parent-gated areas.
- Written to parent-dashboard or parent-settings tone profile.

### Rule: Never Mix Speakers

A mascot should never deliver a system instruction.
A narrator should never have personality.
Parent text should never use baby talk.

## 9. How to Write New Copy That Stays On-Brand

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Identify the audience.** Child or parent?
2. **Identify the context.** Which screen type? Which tone profile?
3. **Check the age group.** What vocabulary level applies?
4. **Write the first draft.** Follow the copy pattern for the screen type.
5. **Run the vocabulary check.** Use `validateCopy()` from `vocabularyLevels.ts`.
6. **Read it aloud.** If it sounds stiff, robotic, or condescending, rewrite.
7. **Check the mascot voice.** If a mascot is speaking, does it sound like THAT
   mascot? Could you identify the mascot from the text alone?
8. **Apply the substitution map.** If the copy targets ages 2--3, substitute
   complex words.
9. **Verify repetition control.** Is this phrase in a variation group? Are there
   enough variants (5+ for common contexts)?

### The "Read Aloud" Test

Every piece of child-facing copy must pass this test:

> Read the text aloud in a warm, friendly voice. If it sounds like something a
> kind preschool teacher would naturally say, it passes. If it sounds like
> something a software product would display, rewrite it.

### The "Opposite Day" Test

Imagine the worst possible version of the text. Then make sure your version is
the opposite.

- Worst: "Error 404: Page not found."
  Best: "Oops! That page took a wrong turn. Let's go back."

- Worst: "Incorrect. The correct answer was B."
  Best: "Almost! The answer is B. You will get it next time."

- Worst: "No content available."
  Best: "Nothing here yet. Your adventure starts now!"

## 10. Review Checklist for Copy Quality

Before any copy ships, it must pass all of the following:

- [ ] **Audience clarity**: Is the text for a child or a parent? Never both.
- [ ] **Age appropriateness**: Validated against vocabulary level for the target age.
- [ ] **Tone match**: Matches the correct tone profile for the context.
- [ ] **Mascot fidelity**: If a mascot speaks, the voice is identifiable.
- [ ] **No forbidden words**: "Wrong," "incorrect," "fail," "bad," "stupid," "hurry."
- [ ] **Sentence length**: Within age-group maximum.
- [ ] **Syllable count**: No word exceeds age-group max syllables.
- [ ] **Active voice**: No passive constructions in child-facing copy.
- [ ] **One idea per sentence**: No stacked instructions.
- [ ] **Read-aloud test**: Sounds natural when spoken by a warm adult.
- [ ] **Positive framing**: Everything framed as what TO do, not what NOT to do.
- [ ] **Variation group**: Phrase exists in a variation group with 5+ alternatives.
- [ ] **No jargon**: Parent text avoids edu-speak; child text avoids tech-speak.
- [ ] **Specificity**: Praise names the action ("You found the red one"), not generic ("Good job").
- [ ] **CTA clarity**: Button text is max 3 words and uses first person or collective voice.
- [ ] **Punctuation rules**: Follows the tone profile's punctuation rules exactly.
- [ ] **Emotional safety**: The text cannot make a child feel bad, ashamed, or rushed.

## 11. Sample Rewrites

Five examples showing the transformation from generic app copy to premium
Kids Learning Fun copy.

### Rewrite 1: A Lesson Intro

**Generic version:**
> "Welcome to the Colors module. In this activity, you will learn to identify
> and name the primary and secondary colors. Click Start to begin the lesson."

**Premium version:**

> **Header**: "Let's learn about colors!"
>
> **Leo (mascot bubble)**: "I love colors. Red, blue, yellow... there are so many
> beautiful ones! Ready to explore them with me?"
>
> **Body**: "In this lesson, you will learn the names of seven colors."
>
> **CTA**: "Let's go!"

**Why it is better:**
- Short, specific header (5 words vs. 6).
- Mascot voice adds personality and warmth.
- "Seven colors" is concrete; "primary and secondary" is jargon.
- "Let's go" is energetic; "Click Start to begin the lesson" is robotic.
- No "module" or "activity" -- those are system words, not child words.

---

### Rewrite 2: A Quiz Wrong Answer

**Generic version:**
> "Incorrect! The correct answer was C) Triangle. Please try the next question."

**Premium version:**

> "Almost! The answer is triangle. See how it has three sides? Let's try the
> next one -- you are doing great."

**Why it is better:**
- "Almost" instead of "Incorrect" -- encouraging, not punishing.
- Teaches in the correction: "See how it has three sides?" turns a miss into
  a micro-lesson.
- "You are doing great" reassures the child that one mistake does not define them.
- No letter labels (C) -- children do not think in multiple-choice notation.
- No "please" in a forced-progression context (it is not really optional).

---

### Rewrite 3: An Empty State

**Generic version:**
> "No data available. Complete activities to populate this section."

**Premium version:**

> **Header**: "Nothing here yet!"
>
> **Body**: "Your adventure starts now. Go explore and come back to see
> what you have collected!"
>
> **CTA**: "Let's explore!"

**Why it is better:**
- "No data available" is system language. "Nothing here yet" is human.
- "Populate this section" is developer speak. "See what you have collected"
  is play language.
- The CTA gives a specific, exciting action instead of leaving the child
  on a dead-end screen.
- The framing turns emptiness into anticipation.

---

### Rewrite 4: A Bedtime Transition

**Generic version:**
> "Bedtime Mode Activated! Tap here to start your nighttime routine.
> Sweet dreams! :)"

**Premium version:**

> **Header**: "Time to wind down..."
>
> **Body**: "The stars are waking up and the moon is smiling down.
> Let us get cozy."
>
> **Ollie (mascot)**: "Goodnight, little one. I will be right here
> in the morning."
>
> **CTA**: "Start bedtime story"

**Why it is better:**
- No exclamation points -- bedtime copy is calm, not excited.
- "Bedtime Mode Activated" sounds like a military operation.
  "Time to wind down..." is a gentle invitation.
- Nature imagery (stars, moon) creates a soothing atmosphere.
- Ollie's message ends with reassurance -- the child is not alone.
- No emoji in the copy -- we use illustration, not text symbols.
- The CTA is soft and specific, not generic "Tap here."

---

### Rewrite 5: A Parent Dashboard Insight

**Generic version:**
> "User Activity Report: The learner has demonstrated improved performance
> across multiple competency domains this reporting period. Engagement metrics
> show a 23% increase in session duration. Recommendation: Continue current
> learning trajectory."

**Premium version:**

> **Summary**: "Emma completed 14 activities this week across 5 topics --
> her busiest week yet."
>
> **Highlight**: "Standout moment: She finished every counting lesson from
> 1 to 20 without a single hint."
>
> **Suggestion**: "She has been loving numbers. Next week, try introducing
> shapes -- they build on the same pattern-recognition skills."

**Why it is better:**
- Uses the child's name, not "the learner" or "the user."
- Leads with a concrete number (14 activities, 5 topics), not a percentage.
- "Her busiest week yet" is warm and contextual; "23% increase in session
  duration" is clinical.
- The highlight names a specific, impressive achievement a parent can
  celebrate with their child at dinner.
- The suggestion explains WHY shapes are a good next step (same underlying
  skill), building parent trust.
- Zero jargon. A parent who has never heard of "competency domains" can
  read this in 10 seconds and feel proud.
